Type of cuts!.

Brisket: Cut from the breast or lower chest of the cow; tough but becomes tender when slow-cooked. Used for BBQ, corned beef, and pot roast. Chuck: Cut from the shoulder area; tough but flavorful. Used for ground beef, pot roasts, and stews. Flank Steak: Cut from the lower abdominal muscles of the cow; lean and full of flavor, best when marinated and grilled or stir-fried. Filet Mignon: A small, premium cut from the tenderloin; the most tender cut of beef, typically grilled or pan-seared. Ribeye: A highly marbled and flavorful cut from the rib section, known for tenderness; popular for grilling.

Type of cuts!.

Sirloin: A lean cut from the back of the cow; less tender than ribeye but still flavorful. Available as top sirloin (tender) and bottom sirloin (cheaper and tougher). Short Ribs: Taken from the rib section; tough but flavorful, often braised or slow-cooked. Skirt Steak: Cut from the diaphragm muscles; known for its rich flavor, often used in fajitas. T-Bone Steak: Combines the tenderloin and striploin cuts, separated by a T-shaped bone. A premium cut, great for grilling.

Type of cuts!.

Tenderloin: The most tender cut of beef, taken from the loin; very lean and best cooked quickly over high heat. Top Round: A lean cut from the hindquarters of the cow; used for roasting, deli meats, or London broil. Tri-Tip: A triangular cut from the sirloin, popular for roasting or grilling, especially in California-style BBQ.

MEAT SUPPLY CHAIN

The meat supply chain is an integrated system involving various stages and stakeholders working together to produce, process, distribute, and market meat products. Below is a more detailed description of each stage and the key components within this chain.

1. Primary Production

Genetic selection: Specific breeds or crossbreeds of animals (cattle, pigs, sheep, poultry) are chosen for their optimal characteristics for meat production, such as fast growth, muscle quality, or disease resistance. Management and feeding: Animals are fed with pasture, grains, concentrates, or balanced diets depending on their species and growth stage. The goal is to ensure healthy growth, improve meat quality, and optimize economic performance. Animal health: Vaccination programs, deworming, and disease control are implemented to ensure animal health and prevent sanitary risks in the meat. Animal welfare: Animals are provided with adequate living conditions that minimize stress, adhering to international or national standards. This is not only an ethical issue but also directly impacts meat quality.

2. Processing and Transformation

Slaughter and butchering: Animals are transported to slaughterhouses or processing plants. Strict protocols are followed to ensure animal welfare during slaughter (e.g., stunning before slaughter). The process includes bleeding, skinning/plucking, and evisceration under hygienic conditions. Cutting and deboning: Different parts of the animal are separated and classified based on the type of meat (lean, fatty) and its commercial use (steaks, chops, ribs). By-products (skin, viscera, bones) are also processed for other purposes, such as pet food or pharmaceuticals. Preservation and storage: Meat is preserved using techniques like refrigeration, freezing, vacuum packaging, or curing, depending on market needs. These measures are essential to maintain quality and prevent contamination. Secondary processing: Meat is transformed into derived products like sausages, ham, pâtés, or marinated meat. This step includes processes like smoking, cooking, fermentation, and packaging

3. Distribution and Marketing

Transportation: Cold chain systems are used to maintain meat at appropriate temperatures and prevent spoilage. Vehicles must comply with sanitary regulations to avoid contamination. Intermediate storage: Products may pass through distribution centers or storage facilities before reaching supermarkets, wholesalers, or butcher shops. Marketing: Products are sold to the final consumer through supermarkets, butcher shops, food stores, or e-commerce platforms. Packaging includes mandatory information such as expiration date, weight, product origin, sanitary certifications, and storage instructions. Certifications and labels: Many products carry quality seals (e.g., organic certification, designation of origin) that add value to the product.

4. Consumption

Culinary preparation: Meat is cooked in various ways (grilled, fried, boiled, smoked, etc.) depending on cultural traditions, personal preferences, and the properties of the cut. Consumers also seek information on cooking methods that preserve flavor and nutritional properties. Consumer preferences: Current trends show a growing preference for high-quality, sustainable, low-fat, or minimally processed products. In some cases, consumers are turning to alternatives to conventional meat, such as lab-grown meat or plant-based products. Food safety: Proper cooking of meat products is recommended to prevent infections from pathogens like Salmonella or Escherichia coli.
